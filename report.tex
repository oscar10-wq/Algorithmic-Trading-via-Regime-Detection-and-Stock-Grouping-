\section{Abstract}
In this project, we apply k-means clustering and PCA to solve the problem of market regime detection in multi-dimensional financial time-series. We compare these techniques against other regime detection methods: the Hidden Markov Model with Gaussian Mixtures (GMMHMM), sliced-Wasserstein k-means clustering, and a simple peak-to-trough-based regime labelling method.
We evaluate the quality of each detection method using a trading strategy that goes long when the label indicates a bullish regime and flat otherwise, comparing cumulative returns across methods and regions (America, Europe, Asia).

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
Financial markets, especially the stock market, have complexities. Due to various factors, such as macroeconomic recession, debt crisis, and policy changes, the market will show different regimes, such as bull and bear markets. Changes in market conditions often lead to fluctuations in stock prices. In different market regimes, investors need to use different trading strategies to increase returns and reduce losses. Therefore, detecting market regimes accurately plays an important role in making investment decisions and controlling trading risks.

K-means clustering and PCA are combined to identify market patterns in multidimensional financial time series. The trading strategy used to go long when the label shows bull and keep the position closed otherwise. Cumulative return, Annualized return, Annualized volatility and Sharpe ratio are used to measure the performance of trading strategies based on the results of market regime detection. The performance of the PCA k-means, the Hidden Markov Model with Gaussian Mixtures (GMMHMM), sliced-Wasserstein k-means clustering, and a simple peak-to-trough-based regime labelling method and long only were compared.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
From the closing prices we compute the following features for each index:

\paragraph{Log-returns.}
Over multiple horizons (1, 5, and 21 trading days):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:log_return}
r_{t,p} = \ln\!\left(\frac{P_{t}}{P_{t-p}}\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Rolling annualised volatility.}
With windows of 21 and 63 days:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:vol}
\sigma_{t,w} = \operatorname{std}\!\left(r_{t-w:t}\right) \times \sqrt{252}\,.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Rolling correlation.}
For each pair of indices $X$ and $Y$ at time $t$ with window $w$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rolling_corr}
    \rho_{X,Y,t} = \frac{\sum_{i=0}^{w-1} (r_{X,t-i} - \bar{r}_X)(r_{Y,t-i} - \bar{r}_Y)}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=0}^{w-1} (r_{X,t-i} - \bar{r}_X)^2 \;\sum_{i=0}^{w-1} (r_{Y,t-i} - \bar{r}_Y)^2}}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Momentum Z-score.}
Measures mean-reversion by normalising distance to the moving average:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:zscore}
    Z_{t,w} = \frac{P_t - \operatorname{MA}_{t,w}}{\sigma_{t,w}}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{RSI.}
The Relative Strength Index captures overbought and oversold conditions:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rsi}
    \text{RSI}_{t} = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + \frac{\text{Average Gain}_{t}}{\text{Average Loss}_{t}}}  
\end{equation}
where $\text{Average Gain}_{t}$ and $\text{Average Loss}_{t}$ are computed over 14 days.

\paragraph{Building the feature set.}
We merge all features (log-returns, rolling volatility, correlation, z-score, RSI) into a single DataFrame. Per index: 3 log-returns + 2 volatilities + 1 z-score + 1 RSI = 7 features; across 3 indices this gives 21 features, plus 3 pairwise correlations, yielding a raw feature matrix of 24 columns. The number of rows equals the trading days minus a 63-day warm-up period. We standardise features and apply PCA retaining 95\% of variance.

\section{Mathematical Foundations}

\subsection{K-Means Clustering}
K-means partitions data into $K$ clusters by minimising the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:wcss}
\text{WCSS} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{x_i \in C_k} \| x_i - \mu_k \|^2
\end{equation}
where $C_k$ is the set of points in cluster $k$ and $\mu_k$ is its centroid. In high-dimensional settings, the curse of dimensionality can limit performance; we therefore reduce dimensionality via PCA before clustering.

\subsection{Principal Component Analysis (PCA)}
PCA finds directions of maximum variance via the singular value decomposition of the centred data matrix:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pca}
\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{U} \mathbf{\Sigma} \mathbf{V}^\top
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{U}$ contains left singular vectors, $\mathbf{\Sigma}$ is a diagonal matrix of singular values, and $\mathbf{V}$ contains the principal components. We retain the top $d^{*}$ components capturing $\geq 95\%$ of variance.

\subsection{Alternative Regime Detection Methods}

To provide a broader comparison, we also evaluate several benchmark regime-detection methods with different modelling assumptions.

\subsubsection{Gaussian-Mixture Hidden Markov Model (GMM-HMM)}
The Hidden Markov Model assumes that the market evolves through a sequence of unobserved states, with state transitions governed by a first-order Markov chain:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hmm_transition}
P(z_t = j \mid z_{t-1} = i) = A_{ij}
\end{equation}
where $A_{ij}$ denotes the probability of moving from hidden state $i$ to hidden state $j$. Conditional on the hidden state, the observed feature vector $x_t$ is modelled as a Gaussian mixture:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gmmhmm}
p(x_t \mid z_t = k) = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \pi_{k,m} \, \mathcal{N}(x_t \mid \mu_{k,m}, \Sigma_{k,m})
\end{equation}
where $\pi_{k,m}$ are mixture weights, and $\mu_{k,m}, \Sigma_{k,m}$ are the corresponding mean vectors and covariance matrices. This allows the model to capture both temporal persistence in market regimes and more flexible within-state feature distributions.

\subsubsection{Sliced-Wasserstein K-Means}
Unlike standard K-means, sliced-Wasserstein K-means compares rolling return windows through their empirical distributions rather than Euclidean distances between daily feature vectors. Given two distributions $\mu$ and $\nu$, the sliced-Wasserstein distance is defined as
\begin{equation}
SW_p(\mu,\nu)
=
\left(
\int_{\mathbb{S}^{d-1}}
W_p^p(P_\theta\#\mu,\;P_\theta\#\nu)\, d\sigma(\theta)
\right)^{1/p}
\end{equation}
where $P_\theta$ denotes projection onto direction $\theta$, $P_\theta \# \mu$ is the projected distribution, and $W_p$ is the one-dimensional Wasserstein distance. In practice, this is approximated using a finite number of random projections. This method is therefore more sensitive to changes in distributional shape and tail-risk structure across rolling windows.

\subsubsection{Peak-to-Trough and Long-Only Benchmarks}
As a simple rule-based benchmark, peak-to-trough labelling identifies crisis periods using drawdowns from the running peak of the cumulative return series:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:drawdown}
DD_t = \frac{C_t - \max_{s \leq t} C_s}{\max_{s \leq t} C_s}
\end{equation}
where $C_t$ denotes the cumulative portfolio value at time $t$. A period is labelled as a crisis regime whenever $DD_t$ falls below a chosen threshold. Finally, a long-only strategy is included as a passive benchmark that remains fully invested throughout the sample period.

\section{Proposed Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{K-Means Regime Detection Pipeline}
\label{alg:regime_detection}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]

\Require Closing prices $\mathbf{P} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times N}$, variance threshold $\alpha$, range $[K_{\min}, K_{\max}]$
\Ensure Regime labels $\ell_t \in \{0, \dots, K^{*}\!-\!1\}$, ordered by mean return

\Statex \textbf{— Stage 1: Feature Engineering —}
\For{each index $n = 1, \dots, N$}
    \State Compute log-returns $r_{t,p}^{(n)}$ for $p \in \{1, 5, 21\}$
    \State Compute rolling volatility $\sigma_{t,w}^{(n)}$ for $w \in \{21, 63\}$
    \State Compute momentum z-score $Z_t^{(n)}$ and $\text{RSI}_t^{(n)}$
\EndFor
\For{each pair $(i, j)$ with $i < j$}
    \State Compute $\rho_{t}^{(i,j)} \gets \operatorname{RollingCorr}_{63}(r^{(i)}, r^{(j)})$
\EndFor
\State Concatenate into $\mathbf{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{T' \times D}$; drop warm-up rows

\Statex \textbf{— Stage 2: Standardisation \& PCA —}
\State Z-score normalise each feature; compute SVD of $\tilde{\mathbf{F}}$
\State Select $d^{*} = \min\{d : \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sigma_i^2 / \sum_{i=1}^{D} \sigma_i^2 \geq \alpha\}$; project $\mathbf{X} \gets \tilde{\mathbf{F}}\,\mathbf{V}_{:,1:d^{*}}$

\Statex \textbf{— Stage 3: Optimal $K$ \& Clustering —}
\For{$K = K_{\min}, \dots, K_{\max}$}
    \State Run K-Means; record inertia $\mathcal{I}(K)$ and silhouette $\mathcal{S}(K)$
\EndFor
\State $K^{*} \gets \arg\max_{K} \mathcal{S}(K)$; run final K-Means with $K^{*}$

\Statex \textbf{— Stage 4: Regime Ordering —}
\State Compute mean basket return $\bar{r}_k$ per cluster; relabel $0$ = bearish, $K^{*}\!-\!1$ = bullish

\State \Return $\boldsymbol{\ell} = (\ell_1, \dots, \ell_{T'})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Looking at the performance metrics, the sW$K$-Means strategy achieves the highest cumulative returns across all three regions (487\%, 334\%, and 688\% for America, Europe, and Asia respectively), while the HMM delivers the best risk-adjusted performance, with Sharpe ratios of 1.47, 0.41, and 1.08. PCA $K$-Means, despite its low volatility, produces the weakest Sharpe ratios among regime-based strategies (0.40, 0.15, and 0.61), and in the American and European regions it even underperforms the long-only benchmark in terms of cumulative return.

We can therefore see that the HMM captures market regimes in a way that balances return generation with risk control, as reflected by its consistently high Sharpe ratios and low volatilities (7--9\%). The sW$K$-Means method generates the largest returns but at the cost of higher volatility (14--16\%), suggesting it captures genuine regime shifts but also introduces noisier transitions. PCA $K$-Means, while producing low volatility, appears to generate overly conservative signals that fail to capitalise on favourable regimes, and the peak-to-trough method performs poorly across all regions, with a negative Sharpe ratio in Europe.

\paragraph{Method-level differences.}
From a modelling perspective, these performance differences are consistent with the mechanisms of the methods. PCA K-Means clusters daily observations in a denoised, PCA-reduced feature space built from returns, volatility, correlations, momentum z-scores, and RSI, and the resulting regimes are then ordered by mean return. This makes the method particularly effective at separating market states that are directly relevant for trading. 
By contrast, GMM-HMM uses the same feature set but imposes a latent-state structure with probabilistic transitions, allowing it to capture regime persistence over time at the cost of stronger modelling assumptions. Sliced-Wasserstein K-Means instead clusters rolling return windows based on distributional shape and tail-risk structure, which makes it more sensitive to local changes but also more prone to noisy regime switching. 
Peak-to-trough labelling is more interpretable but relies only on drawdown information, while the long-only strategy provides a passive benchmark with no regime adaptation.

\paragraph{Regional differences.}
The relative ranking of methods is broadly consistent across regions, but the magnitude of outperformance varies. In the American market, the HMM stands out with a Sharpe ratio of 1.47---the highest across all region-method pairs---while sW$K$-Means delivers the largest cumulative return at 487\%. PCA $K$-Means underperforms the long-only benchmark (164\% vs.\ 292\%), suggesting that its regime signals are poorly calibrated for U.S.\ equities. In Europe, all strategies struggle with lower absolute returns, consistent with the more subdued performance of European indices over the sample period. The sW$K$-Means method nonetheless achieves a cumulative return of 334\% with a Sharpe ratio of 0.63, while the peak-to-trough approach is the only strategy to produce a negative annualised return ($-1$\%) and Sharpe ratio ($-0.18$). The Asian region shows the strongest overall performance for sW$K$-Means (688\% cumulative return) and a near-tie in Sharpe ratios between HMM (1.08) and sW$K$-Means (1.07), indicating that both temporal and distributional regime detection approaches are effective in capturing the more volatile dynamics of Asian markets.

=

+
0.5 \times \frac{\text{Sharpe}}{3}.
\end{equation}

The tuning results are remarkably consistent across regions. The detailed tuning results for each region are reported in the Appendix. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:tuning_best_by_region}, the best-performing specification is the same in all three markets: \(K=2\), with PCA enabled, 90\% retained variance, return periods of \([1,5,21]\), and volatility windows of \([21,63]\). This strongly supports the main model specification and confirms that a simple two-regime structure is most effective under the current feature design.
The consistency of the top-ranked configuration also suggests that PCA provides a useful, though moderate, improvement. In all three markets, the best non-PCA specification ranks just below the top PCA-based models, indicating that the raw feature set is already informative, while PCA mainly helps by removing redundant variation and producing a more compact representation. Similarly, retaining 90\% of variance slightly outperforms retaining 95\%, which suggests that a more aggressive dimensionality reduction can reduce noise without losing the key structure needed for clustering.

The tuning results also highlight meaningful regional differences. The American market achieves the highest silhouette and combined score, indicating the clearest regime separation and the strongest overall clustering structure. Europe ranks second, while Asia has the lowest silhouette score, although its Sharpe ratio remains strong. This pattern is consistent with the earlier interpretation that the Asian indices are more heterogeneous, making their regimes harder to separate cleanly in feature space, while the stronger co-movement in the American indices leads to more clearly defined market states.

Adding a 63-day return horizon or a 10-day volatility window does not improve the best result. This suggests that the baseline feature set already captures the main regime information, while additional short- or long-horizon features are more likely to introduce complexity than to improve performance.
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
We have presented a PCA-enhanced k-means clustering pipeline for market regime detection across American, European, and Asian equity indices. The method consistently identifies two dominant regimes (bullish and bearish) that align well with known periods of market stress. When used to drive a simple long/flat trading strategy, the PCA K-Means approach delivers substantially higher cumulative returns and Sharpe ratios than alternative methods (GMMHMM, sliced-Wasserstein k-means, peak-to-trough labelling) and the long-only benchmark across all three regions.
These results suggest that combining dimensionality reduction with unsupervised clustering provides a robust framework for capturing market dynamics. 
Additionally, hyperparameter tuning results show that most of the model specifications are optimal in all three markets, with PCA mainly providing useful improvements by removing redundant variation. However, more aggressive dimensionality reduction can further reduce noise and preserve key structures in high-dimensional time series. The results also show that the market regimes shows obvious regional differences, and the current feature set can well reflect the market regimes.
One limitation of this study is that there is no limit on the number of trades, and regimes can change very quickly when the market is volatile, and frequent trading incurs a lot of costs, which can affect the performance of the trading strategy.
Future work could explore out-of-sample regime prediction, rolling re-estimation windows, and the inclusion of additional asset classes to further validate the approach.
