\section{Abstract}
In this project, we apply k-means clustering and PCA to solve the problem of market regime detection in multi-dimensional financial time-series. We compare these techniques against other regime detection methods: the Hidden Markov Model with Gaussian Mixtures (GMMHMM), sliced-Wasserstein k-means clustering, and a simple peak-to-trough-based regime labelling method.
We evaluate the quality of each detection method using a trading strategy that goes long when the label indicates a bullish regime and flat otherwise, comparing cumulative returns across methods and regions (America, Europe, Asia).

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
Financial markets, especially the stock market, have complexities. Due to various factors, such as macroeconomic recession, debt crisis, and policy changes, the market will show different regimes, such as bull and bear markets. Changes in market conditions often lead to fluctuations in stock prices. In different market regimes, investors need to use different trading strategies to increase returns and reduce losses. Therefore, detecting market regimes accurately plays an important role in making investment decisions and controlling trading risks. Traditional means of regime classification use a rules-based definition, such as the peak-to-trough regime labelling method. In contrast, unsupervised learning models identify regimes endogenously from the data, allowing for a more flexible and data-driven characterisation of market states.

K-means clustering and PCA are combined to identify market patterns in multidimensional financial time series. The trading strategy used to go long when the label shows bull and keep the position closed otherwise. Cumulative return, Annualized return, Annualized volatility and Sharpe ratio are used to measure the performance of trading strategies based on the results of market regime detection. The performance of the PCA k-means, the Hidden Markov Model with Gaussian Mixtures (GMMHMM), sliced-Wasserstein k-means clustering, and a simple peak-to-trough-based regime labelling method and long only strategy are compared in this project.
In particular we fit our models on the period of 2010 to 2020 and we test the data on the period 2020 to 2024. We then compare the cumulative returns of the different strategies on the test set.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
From the closing prices we compute the following features for each index:

\paragraph{Log-returns.}
Over multiple horizons (1, 5, and 21 trading days):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:log_return}
r_{t,p} = \ln\!\left(\frac{P_{t}}{P_{t-p}}\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Rolling annualised volatility.}
With windows of 21 and 63 days:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:vol}
\sigma_{t,w} = \operatorname{std}\!\left(r_{t-w:t}\right) \times \sqrt{252}\,.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Rolling correlation.}
For each pair of indices $X$ and $Y$ at time $t$ with window $w$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rolling_corr}
    \rho_{X,Y,t} = \frac{\sum_{i=0}^{w-1} (r_{X,t-i} - \bar{r}_X)(r_{Y,t-i} - \bar{r}_Y)}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=0}^{w-1} (r_{X,t-i} - \bar{r}_X)^2 \;\sum_{i=0}^{w-1} (r_{Y,t-i} - \bar{r}_Y)^2}}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Momentum Z-score.}
Measures mean-reversion by normalising distance to the moving average:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:zscore}
    Z_{t,w} = \frac{P_t - \operatorname{MA}_{t,w}}{\sigma_{t,w}}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{RSI.}
The Relative Strength Index captures overbought and oversold conditions:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rsi}
    \text{RSI}_{t} = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + \frac{\text{Average Gain}_{t}}{\text{Average Loss}_{t}}}  
\end{equation}
where $\text{Average Gain}_{t}$ and $\text{Average Loss}_{t}$ are computed over 14 days.

\paragraph{Building the feature set.}
We merge all features (log-returns, rolling volatility, correlation, z-score, RSI) into a single DataFrame. Per index: 3 log-returns + 2 volatilities + 1 z-score + 1 RSI = 7 features; across 3 indices this gives 21 features, plus 3 pairwise correlations, yielding a raw feature matrix of 24 columns. The number of rows equals the trading days minus a 63-day warm-up period. We standardise features and apply PCA retaining 95\% of variance.

\section{Mathematical Foundations}

\subsection{K-Means Clustering}
K-means partitions data into $K$ clusters by minimising the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:wcss}
\text{WCSS} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{x_i \in C_k} \| x_i - \mu_k \|^2
\end{equation}
where $C_k$ is the set of points in cluster $k$ and $\mu_k$ is its centroid. In high-dimensional settings, the curse of dimensionality can limit performance; we therefore reduce dimensionality via PCA before clustering.

\subsection{Principal Component Analysis (PCA)}
PCA finds directions of maximum variance via the singular value decomposition of the centred data matrix:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pca}
\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{U} \mathbf{\Sigma} \mathbf{V}^\top
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{U}$ contains left singular vectors, $\mathbf{\Sigma}$ is a diagonal matrix of singular values, and $\mathbf{V}$ contains the principal components. We retain the top $d^{*}$ components capturing $\geq 95\%$ of variance.

\subsection{Alternative Regime Detection Methods}

To provide a broader comparison, we also evaluate several benchmark regime-detection methods with different modelling assumptions.

\subsubsection{Gaussian-Mixture Hidden Markov Model (GMM-HMM)}
The Hidden Markov Model assumes that the market evolves through a sequence of unobserved states, with state transitions governed by a first-order Markov chain:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hmm_transition}
P(z_t = j \mid z_{t-1} = i) = A_{ij}
\end{equation}
where $A_{ij}$ denotes the probability of moving from hidden state $i$ to hidden state $j$. Conditional on the hidden state, the observed feature vector $x_t$ is modelled as a Gaussian mixture:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gmmhmm}
p(x_t \mid z_t = k) = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \pi_{k,m} \, \mathcal{N}(x_t \mid \mu_{k,m}, \Sigma_{k,m})
\end{equation}
where $\pi_{k,m}$ are mixture weights, and $\mu_{k,m}, \Sigma_{k,m}$ are the corresponding mean vectors and covariance matrices. This allows the model to capture both temporal persistence in market regimes and more flexible within-state feature distributions.

\subsubsection{Sliced-Wasserstein K-Means}
Unlike standard K-means, sliced-Wasserstein K-means compares rolling return windows through their empirical distributions rather than Euclidean distances between daily feature vectors. Given two distributions $\mu$ and $\nu$, the sliced-Wasserstein distance is defined as
\begin{equation}
SW_p(\mu,\nu)
=
\left(
\int_{\mathbb{S}^{d-1}}
W_p^p(P_\theta\#\mu,\;P_\theta\#\nu)\, d\sigma(\theta)
\right)^{1/p}
\end{equation}
where $P_\theta$ denotes projection onto direction $\theta$, $P_\theta \# \mu$ is the projected distribution, and $W_p$ is the one-dimensional Wasserstein distance. In practice, this is approximated using a finite number of random projections. This method is therefore more sensitive to changes in distributional shape and tail-risk structure across rolling windows.

\subsubsection{Peak-to-Trough and Long-Only Benchmarks}
As a simple rule-based benchmark, peak-to-trough labelling identifies crisis periods using drawdowns from the running peak of the cumulative return series:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:drawdown}
DD_t = \frac{C_t - \max_{s \leq t} C_s}{\max_{s \leq t} C_s}
\end{equation}
where $C_t$ denotes the cumulative portfolio value at time $t$. A period is labelled as a crisis regime whenever $DD_t$ falls below a chosen threshold. Finally, a long-only strategy is included as a passive benchmark that remains fully invested throughout the sample period.

\section{Proposed Algorithm}
Here we present a detailed algorithmic outline of the PCA K-Means regime detection pipeline, summarising the key steps from raw data processing to regime labelling and ordering.
\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{K-Means Regime Detection Pipeline}
\label{alg:regime_detection}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]

\Require Closing prices $\mathbf{P} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times N}$, variance threshold $\alpha$, range $[K_{\min}, K_{\max}]$
\Ensure Regime labels $\ell_t \in \{0, \dots, K^{*}\!-\!1\}$, ordered by mean return

\Statex \textbf{— Stage 1: Feature Engineering —}
\For{each index $n = 1, \dots, N$}
    \State Compute log-returns $r_{t,p}^{(n)}$ for $p \in \{1, 5, 21\}$
    \State Compute rolling volatility $\sigma_{t,w}^{(n)}$ for $w \in \{21, 63\}$
    \State Compute momentum z-score $Z_t^{(n)}$ and $\text{RSI}_t^{(n)}$
\EndFor
\For{each pair $(i, j)$ with $i < j$}
    \State Compute $\rho_{t}^{(i,j)} \gets \operatorname{RollingCorr}_{63}(r^{(i)}, r^{(j)})$
\EndFor
\State Concatenate into $\mathbf{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{T' \times D}$; drop warm-up rows

\Statex \textbf{— Stage 2: Standardisation \& PCA —}
\State Z-score normalise each feature; compute SVD of $\tilde{\mathbf{F}}$
\State Select $d^{*} = \min\{d : \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sigma_i^2 / \sum_{i=1}^{D} \sigma_i^2 \geq \alpha\}$; project $\mathbf{X} \gets \tilde{\mathbf{F}}\,\mathbf{V}_{:,1:d^{*}}$

\Statex \textbf{— Stage 3: Optimal $K$ \& Clustering —}
\For{$K = K_{\min}, \dots, K_{\max}$}
    \State Run K-Means; record inertia $\mathcal{I}(K)$ and silhouette $\mathcal{S}(K)$
\EndFor
\State $K^{*} \gets \arg\max_{K} \mathcal{S}(K)$; run final K-Means with $K^{*}$

\Statex \textbf{— Stage 4: Regime Ordering —}
\State Compute mean basket return $\bar{r}_k$ per cluster; relabel $0$ = bearish, $K^{*}\!-\!1$ = bullish

\State \Return $\boldsymbol{\ell} = (\ell_1, \dots, \ell_{T'})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Optimal $K$ Selection}
We applied k-means for $K \in \{2, \dots, 10\}$ on the PCA-reduced feature space and computed inertia and silhouette scores. The silhouette score is maximised at $K=2$ across all regions, suggesting two dominant regimes over 2010--2020. This result suggests that, given the sample period and feature design adopted in this study, market states are primarily characterised by two dominant structures: a relatively weak bearish regime and a relatively strong bullish regime. Relative to larger values of $K$, the two-regime specification achieves better cluster separation while avoiding the introduction of excessive noise and potential overfitting. It also preserves the stability and interpretability of the detected regimes.

We used the 95\% variance threshold for PCA and we used Return periods of 1, 5, and 21 days, rolling volatilities with windows of 21 and 63 days, momentum z-score with a window of 21 days and RSI as features for the PCA K-Means model.

On the test set the training strategies start with an inital capital of \$ 100 at the 1st of January 2020 and we calculate the cumulative returns until the 1st of January 2024.
We shift the signal by one day to avoid look-ahead bias, and we do not apply transaction costs or slippage in this backtest.

Looking at the performance metrics, the sW$K$-Means strategy achieves the highest cumulative returns across regions except the Asian region (169.39\%, 216.95\%, 117.90\% for America, Europe, and Asia respectively). 
The HMM strategy delivers consistently strong risk adjusted performance in our framework. However, our main PCA K-Means strategy underperforms the long-only benchmark in America and Asia, and it has a negative Sharpe ratio in Europe and Asia, indicating that its regime signals are not effectively capturing profitable trading opportunities in these regions. 
The peak-to-trough method is worse than the PCA K-Means methods only for the European market but still outperforms our model for the American and Asian markets. 

We can therefore see that the PCA K-Means methods, while conceptually appealing and interpretable, do not perform as well as more complex methods that either capture temporal dependencies (HMM) or distributional changes (sW$K$-Means). This suggests that the regimes detected by PCA K-Means may be too noisy or not sufficiently aligned with market conditions that drive returns, while the more sophisticated methods are better able to extract meaningful regime signals from the data.
Its underperformance against long-only benchmarks in the three regions, indicates this major flaw in its regime detection capacity. 

\paragraph{Regional differences.}
The PCA K-Means strategy performs relatively better in the American market, achieving a positive Sharpe ratio. In contrast, it underperforms in Europe and Asia, with negative Sharpe ratios. This pattern may reflect differences in market structure and co-movement across regions. The American indices are more tightly correlated, which may lead to clearer regime separation in feature space, while the Asian indices are more heterogeneous, making regime detection more challenging.

\begin{equation}

=
0.5 \times \text{Silhouette}
+
0.5 \times \frac{\text{Sharpe}}{3}.
\end{equation}

The tuning results are remarkably consistent across regions. The detailed tuning results for each region are reported in the Appendix. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:tuning_best_by_region}, the best-performing specification is the same in all three markets: \(K=2\), with PCA enabled, 90\% retained variance, return periods of \([1,5,21]\), and volatility windows of \([21,63]\). This strongly supports the main model specification and confirms that a simple two-regime structure is most effective under the current feature design.
The consistency of the top-ranked configuration also suggests that PCA provides a useful, though moderate, improvement. In all three markets, the best non-PCA specification ranks just below the top PCA-based models, indicating that the raw feature set is already informative, while PCA mainly helps by removing redundant variation and producing a more compact representation. Similarly, retaining 90\% of variance slightly outperforms retaining 95\%, which suggests that a more aggressive dimensionality reduction can reduce noise without losing the key structure needed for clustering.

The tuning results also highlight meaningful regional differences. The American market achieves the highest silhouette and combined score, indicating the clearest regime separation and the strongest overall clustering structure. Europe ranks second, while Asia has the lowest silhouette score, although its Sharpe ratio remains strong. This pattern is consistent with the earlier interpretation that the Asian indices are more heterogeneous, making their regimes harder to separate cleanly in feature space, while the stronger co-movement in the American indices leads to more clearly defined market states.

Adding a 63-day return horizon or a 10-day volatility window does not improve the best result. This suggests that the baseline feature set already captures the main regime information, while additional short- or long-horizon features are more likely to introduce complexity than to improve performance.
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Limitations}
Our analysis is limited in several key ways. Firstly, all model classification performance is assessed on the results from the trading strategy. Whilst this is a useful proxy, it assumes that true regime classification translates to superior trading performance. In future work, the model performance could be tested using alternative methods. For instance, testing the models' regime classifiers against traditional rules-based methodologies such as drawdown or peak-to-trough labelling, allowing classification accuracy to be tested more directly. 

Moreover, all trading performance was measured in the absence of transaction costs. This is an unrealistic expectation, and may have overstated the performance of models which frequently switch regimes. For future work, modelling these costs, such as bid-ask spreads with the Roll Model, may provide a more realistic assessment of model performance.

Another limitation concerns the lack of depth for training and testing of the models. We tested on a four-year period from 2020 to 2024. Although this was a highly volatile and varied period (e.g. COVID and subsequent recovery), a four-year window for testing limits the reliability and robustness of results. In future, increasing the size of the testing and training window would offer greater insight into the performance of the models. In addition, evaluating performance in varying market conditions would give more understanding on how the models adapt to different enviroments.

\section{Conclusion}
We have presented a PCA-enhanced k-means clustering pipeline for market regime detection across American, European, and Asian equity indices. The method identifies two dominant regimes (bullish and bearish) that align relatively well with known periods of market stress. However, when used to drive a simple long/flat trading strategy in out-of-sample test data, the PCA K-Means approach underperforms  compared to alternative methods (GMMHMM, sliced-Wasserstein k-means, peak-to-trough labelling) and the long-only benchmark across all three regions.
These results suggest that combining dimensionality reduction with unsupervised clustering provides a robust framework for capturing market dynamics. 
Additionally, hyperparameter tuning results show that most of the model specifications are optimal in all three markets, with PCA mainly providing useful improvements by removing redundant variation. However, more aggressive dimensionality reduction can further reduce noise and preserve key structures in high-dimensional time series. The results also show that the market regimes shows obvious regional differences, and the current feature set can well reflect the market regimes.
One limitation of this study is that there is no limit on the number of trades, and regimes can change very quickly when the market is volatile. 
Additionally, frequent trading incurs a lot of costs, which can affect the performance of the trading strategy.

\subsection{Reflection on Team Work}
For this project, our team focussed on evaluating machine learning techniques on regime detection, with a primary focus on K-means and PCA. We settled on this project after a group polling system where various ideas were suggested, before selecting the majority vote. This ensured that the motivation for the group work  was not driven by a single individual.

We split the work into several sections: model selection and coding, backtesting, hyperparameter selection, project write up and overall review. Tasks were then distributed based on individual preferences and skillsets (eg. coding ability, machine-learning knowledge, write-up ability) to assign work in the most effective way and in areas where members were most comfortable.

We utilised Teams to discuss what work had been performed and what required future attention. Meetings were conducted frequently, to track progress and align on next steps. Individual roles were tweaked and refined as workload evolved.
In addition, we set up a WhatsApp group for discussion of specifics and individual queries. This enabled quick clarification of issues without requiring full meetings. A GitHub repository was also set up to provide a centralised workspace. This ensured each individual could work on up-to-date versions of the material, and live changes could be observed.

The team worked effectively together, and the individual contributions were well divided. In addition, the democratic selection for the project ensured that motivation and participation were high throughout.

Occasionally, competing coursework deadlines created a slight workload imbalance in the team, although this did not signifcantly impact overall progress. For future, an explicit review of deadlines should be conducted at the start of the project, to aid planning and workload distribution more efficiently.
That said, overall contribution from all members was fair and satisfactory.

One challenge we did encounter was the finding of unreasonably strong trading returns. Subsequent analysis found some of the code had incorporated a form of look-ahead bias. The models had been trained on future data, creating falsely optimistic results. This was immediately notified to all members, and the data pipeline was corrected. Ultimately, trading returns and performance metrics(eg. Sharpe) began to show more realistic outcomes.

Overall, the team are happy with the outcome of the project and satisfied with the work of each individual.
